Items

靈玉 Spirit Jade

Spirit Jade, usually in coins of 50 coins to a lb, can be used to restore spell slots over time. Once the coins are consumed, they become grey like any other rock. Spirit jade comes in tiers: low, medium, high, pristine; low is common, medium is rare, and the other two are near priceless.

Nominally, 100 lb of low level is worth 1 lb of medium, etc. One low jade coin is worth around 1 tael of silver. By default when prices are in spirit jade, the low level is assumed.

If 1/10th of the required amount of jade is used, but that jade is one tier higher, then the recovery time is divided by 10. (A second level spell would take 1 hour and 50 low-level coins to recover, but only 6 minutes with 5 mid-level coins). Below are shown minimum requirements.

6th 100 mid level
7th 10 high level
8th 100 high level
9th 1 pristine level

Chinese

So, naturally, this is a Xianxia setting, where the common language will be some sort of Chinese. There are certain pronouns and honorifics which are somewhat difficult to translate, so this will be kind of a brief vocabulary guide. Tones are omitted for convenience.

Yale Romanisation

Yale romanisation of Mandarin is a romanisation system created in the 60s in Yale University, and is one of the best systems for english speakers specifically. I will use a (slighly modified) Yale romansiation so you don't have to learn what those funny letters sound like.

Some notes though, because it's not perfect:

  • Pronounce all the letters as you'd see them in english - j and ch are hard, not soft (~ y, sh).
  • The ou digraph makes an "oh" sound.

Honorifics and Titles

There is a (very) heavy emphasis on 敬語 (jing yu, respectful language) and 謙語 (chian yu, self-deprecating language) in chinese. Analogous to a servant calling their lord "My Lord" or "Your Excellence" and themselves "Your Servant", they tend to be a little more widespread and also kinda difficult to translate nicely into regular english (especially the 謙语). Generally I will try translate as appropriate, but for reference the following are given below.

Honorifics and titles (like "Mr") come after the surname. "Mr" is translated to 先生 (syian sheng), though this is actually technically a gender neutral term. Mr Wu (吳) would be 吳先生 (Wu Syiansheng).

Often in chinese familial titles (e.g. brother, sister, uncle, auntie, etc) are used to refer to friends, and fellow disciples of a sect. In essence, they are your family, just not necessarily blood family.

One keyword to note is 阿姨 a-yi which is to chinese what babushka is to russian.

Honorifics

To refer to the adresser (me):

  • 在下 (dzai syia)
  • 晚輩 (wan bei); (when talking to someone senior)
  • 小人 (syiao ren); (lit. small/mean person)
  • 貧道 (pin dao); (lit. poor daoist, from a daoist priest)

To refer to the adresee (you):

  • 閣下 (ge syia)
  • 足下 (dzu syia)
  • 前輩 (chian bei); (adressing someone senior)

Titles

These might be more commonly left untranslated. All of these are given with the surname 吳 (Wu) for reference.

  • 吳道友 (- dao you); cultivation friend, way cultivators address each other
  • 吳仙師 (- syian shr); immortal master, way commonfolk address cultivators
  • 吳道長 (- dao jang); master taoist, title of taoist priests
